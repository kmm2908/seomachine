How Often Should You Get a Massage: A Guide to the Right Frequency

How often you should get a massage depends on your health goals, lifestyle, and what your body is telling you.

There is no single answer that works for everyone. But research and clinical experience point to clear patterns: more frequent sessions for pain, injury, or chronic stress, and monthly maintenance for general wellness. Knowing those patterns helps you build a routine that delivers lasting results, not just occasional relief.

Massage is, as described in the Wikipedia article on massage, the rubbing or kneading of the body's soft tissues to treat stress or pain. What fewer people understand is that its effects are cumulative. A single session does good. A regular schedule does significantly more.

Why Frequency Matters More Than Most People Think

Massage works on both the muscles and the nervous system. Regular sessions lower cortisol, the body's main stress hormone, while triggering the release of endorphins, which lift mood and reduce pain. These effects are real and measurable. But they don't last after a single treatment.

Think of massage the way you think of exercise. One session produces a response. Regular sessions produce a lasting shift.

When appointments are too far apart, tension and stress rebuild before the next visit. You end up undoing the same damage each time, rather than building on progress.

How Often to Get a Massage for General Wellness

For people without a specific injury or long-term condition, once every three to four weeks is a strong starting point. At this rate, muscle health is maintained and stress is managed before it builds.

Desk workers are a clear example. Sitting for long hours puts sustained strain on the neck, upper back, and shoulders. A monthly session — such as a Thai Oil Massage or Swedish massage — helps reverse that tension before it becomes chronic pain.

You can book your session online and treat it the same way you'd treat the gym: something you do regularly, not just when things get bad.

Recommended Massage Frequency by Goal

Different goals call for different schedules. Here is a clear breakdown based on clinical guidance and research:

  • Stress and anxiety management: Once or twice a month at minimum. Those with high daily stress may benefit from weekly sessions.
  • Chronic pain (back pain, neck pain, tension headaches): Weekly or every two weeks to start, reducing to once every three to four weeks as symptoms improve. A 2014 study found that two to three 60-minute sessions per week gave greater benefit for neck pain than a single weekly session.
  • Sports recovery and athletic performance: Weekly or every two weeks during training. In peak blocks or before major events, some athletes benefit from more than one session per week.
  • Injury rehabilitation: One to two sessions per week in the early phase, reducing as recovery progresses.
  • General maintenance and relaxation: Once a month is enough for most people with low to moderate stress and no specific condition.
  • Sleep improvement: Once or twice a month. Regular massage has been shown to improve sleep quality, especially for those carrying chronic stress.

What to Expect After Changing Your Frequency

If you are new to massage therapy, or returning after a long break, you may need a "front-loading" phase. This means more frequent sessions at the start to address built-up tension, followed by a lighter maintenance schedule once your body has responded. Your therapist will guide you based on how you feel after each treatment.

It is also worth knowing that a gap of at least 48 hours between deep massage sessions is wise. The body needs time to recover after deep soft tissue work.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is once a month enough to feel the benefits of massage?

For most people with moderate stress and no specific health condition, once a month is a solid rhythm. It keeps muscle tension in check, supports circulation, and provides a regular stress reset. If your job is physically demanding or you carry high levels of stress, you may need sessions every two to three weeks to get the same level of relief.

Can you get a massage too often?

For most massage styles, daily sessions are not needed and can leave muscles feeling overworked. A gap of at least 48 hours between deep tissue or sports massage sessions is generally advised. Gentler treatments like aromatherapy or Swedish massage can be enjoyed more often without the same concern. Your therapist will advise on the right spacing for your treatment.

How often should you get a massage for chronic back pain?

For chronic back pain, weekly or fortnightly sessions are usually recommended at the start, reducing as symptoms improve. Research supports regular massage for low back pain, with benefits that can last well beyond the treatment period when sessions are kept up. Talk to your therapist about adjusting the schedule as your condition gets better.

Does massage frequency change if you exercise regularly?

Yes. Active people and athletes tend to benefit from more frequent massage because exercise puts repeated stress on muscles during recovery. Weekly or fortnightly sessions help maintain flexibility, range of motion, and recovery speed. During peak training, some athletes book every few days. Sports massage, which targets the specific muscle groups used in training, works well in this context.

How soon will you notice results from regular massage therapy?

Many people feel a clear improvement in tension and mood after just one session. Longer-term benefits — such as lasting pain relief, better posture, and improved sleep — tend to show after three to four consistent sessions. Think of the first few appointments as laying a foundation. Each session after that builds on what came before.

What type of massage is best for regular maintenance sessions?

It depends on what your body needs. Traditional Thai massage uses assisted stretching and acupressure along energy pathways called sen lines. It works well for maintaining flexibility and releasing deep postural tension. Swedish massage uses flowing strokes to support circulation and relaxation, making it a good fit for those focused on stress management. Your therapist can help you choose the right treatment based on your goals and how your body responds over time.
