If you are living with backache, you already know how much it takes from you.

The persistent ache when you stand up from your desk. The stiffness first thing in the morning. The way it quietly undermines everything from your focus at work to your sleep at night. Thai massage and targeted massage therapy offer one of the most effective, evidence-backed ways to address the muscle tension and postural strain behind the vast majority of back pain cases.

What Is Backache?

Back pain affects an estimated nine out of ten adults at some point in their lives. It covers pain felt anywhere along the spine, from the base of the neck to the tailbone. The lower back is the most commonly affected area, and pain can range from a dull, steady ache to a sharp jolt that stops you mid-step.

Most backache has a mechanical cause. Muscles, ligaments, and soft tissue around the spine become strained, inflamed, or out of balance. Prolonged sitting, poor posture, repeated movement, and stress are among the most common triggers.

NHS Inform's guidance on back problems notes that symptoms can include hot, burning, or shooting pain. In some cases, nerve irritation causes pain that travels into the legs and feet.

How Massage Helps Backache

A Cochrane review on PubMed examining massage for low back pain found that massage beat sham treatment for both pain and function. The benefits lasted at least a year after treatment ended. That is a strong result, and it reflects what skilled massage does to the tissues involved.

Swedish massage uses long, flowing strokes and graduated pressure. It boosts blood flow to the affected muscles and reduces the local inflammation that drives chronic aching.

Sports massage goes deeper, working into muscle layers to break down adhesions and restore healthy tissue function. For back pain rooted in postural strain and muscle imbalance, this targeted approach can deliver significant relief.

Traditional Thai massage adds another layer through its assisted stretching technique. It lengthens shortened muscles along the posterior chain, restoring range of motion through the hips, glutes, and lower back.

Hot stone massage also has a clear role in back pain treatment. The warmth of basalt stones reaches deeper into muscle than hand pressure alone. This speeds up the release of tension that has built up over months or years.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can massage make backache worse?

In most cases, no. Mild soreness lasting 24 to 48 hours after treatment is normal, especially after deeper work on muscles that have been tense for a long time. Your therapist will work within your comfort level and adjust pressure based on your feedback.

If you have an acute injury, a herniated disc, or nerve pain running down your leg, speak to your GP before booking a massage.

How many sessions will I need to see a difference in my back pain?

Many clients notice a clear drop in tension and pain after a single session. For chronic or recurring backache, a course of four to six sessions spaced one to two weeks apart tends to produce lasting results.

The Cochrane review of massage for low back pain found that benefits lasted at least a year after a course of treatment.

Which type of massage is best for lower back pain?

It depends on the cause and nature of your pain. Swedish massage works well for general tension and poor circulation. Sports massage targets deeper muscle adhesions. Traditional Thai massage tackles postural imbalance through assisted stretching. Hot stone massage suits chronic, deeply held tension.

Your therapist at Serendipity will assess which approach, or combination of approaches, is right for your back.

Is massage safe if my back pain is long-standing or recurring?

Yes, for most people with non-specific chronic back pain. Massage is widely recognised as a safe therapy for muscle and joint back pain. It sits alongside physiotherapy in NHS guidance on manual therapy for back conditions.

If you have been diagnosed with osteoporosis, a spinal fracture, or inflammatory arthritis, please talk to your therapist and GP before booking.

Should I see a GP before coming for a massage for backache?

If your back pain started after an accident, or comes with bladder or bowel changes, severe leg weakness, or unexplained weight loss, see your GP first.

For the vast majority of common backache — including tension-related, postural, and stress-driven pain — you do not need a GP referral to book a therapeutic massage.

Can regular massage help prevent backache from returning?

Yes. Keeping soft tissue healthy through regular massage reduces the chance of tension building to the point of pain. Many clients at Serendipity book monthly sessions to keep the posterior chain mobile and avoid the build-up that triggers acute episodes.

Combined with stretching and regular movement, massage is one of the most effective long-term strategies for managing back pain.
