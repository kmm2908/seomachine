How Often Should You Get a Massage? A Practical Frequency Guide

How often you should get a massage depends on your goals, lifestyle, and what your body is telling you.

There is no single answer that works for everyone, but research and practice offer clear guidance. Whether you are trying traditional Thai massage for the first time or building a routine around sports recovery, knowing how often to book is the first step toward real results.

Why Massage Frequency Matters

Massage, as described in the Wikipedia article on Massage, is the rubbing or kneading of the body's soft tissues. It is most often used to treat stress or pain. The key word is treatment — and like most therapies, how often you have it shapes how well it works.

A single session can ease tension and improve circulation. But the real gains — less muscle tightness, better range of motion, lower stress levels — build up over time. One massage a year is better than none. A steady schedule is better still.

General Wellness and Stress Management

For most people dealing with everyday stress, desk tension, or general muscle tightness, a session every two to four weeks is a good starting point. This helps stop the kind of tension that builds slowly and becomes harder to shift.

If your stress is high — long hours, a physical job, or ongoing pressure — weekly massage will make a clear difference. For lighter needs, once a month is a sensible routine that keeps the body in a better state. You can book a session online and adjust from there.

Chronic Pain and Injury Recovery

When massage targets a specific condition, the approach changes. Research cited by Healthline found that two to three 60-minute sessions per week gave better results for neck pain than one weekly session of the same length. For lower back pain, weekly sessions over ten weeks showed lasting improvement after the course ended.

This "front-loading" approach — more sessions at the start, fewer as things improve — works well for back pain, sciatica, and post-injury recovery. A Thai sports massage, which focuses on recovery and injury prevention, fits well into this kind of plan.

  • Acute injury or chronic pain: Weekly or twice-weekly sessions for the first four to six weeks, then reduce as symptoms ease
  • Lower back pain: Weekly sessions over an eight to ten week course have shown lasting improvement in research
  • Neck and shoulder tension: Two to three sessions per week at first, stepping down to fortnightly maintenance

Sports Performance and Athletic Recovery

For anyone training regularly, massage frequency should match training load. During a normal training period, one session every two to three weeks supports recovery and lowers injury risk. Around peak training or competition, some athletes benefit from weekly or twice-weekly sessions.

Deep tissue massage targets the deeper layers of muscle using slow strokes and firm pressure. Research has found it can be as effective as anti-inflammatory drugs for lower back pain. For athletes, it is a strong recovery tool that supports training rather than getting in the way of it.

What to Consider When Choosing Your Frequency

There is no set rule, but these are the key things to think about before settling on a schedule.

  • Your goals: Relaxation needs less frequency than pain management or athletic recovery
  • Your body's response: If soreness lasts more than two days after a session, allow more time between appointments
  • Session type: Deep tissue and sports massage are more intense — allow at least one to two weeks between these
  • Budget and schedule: A realistic monthly routine does more good than the occasional intensive burst

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should you get a Thai massage specifically?

For general wellbeing and flexibility, every two to four weeks works well for most people. If you are dealing with chronic tension, poor posture, or stress, starting with weekly sessions for a month and then moving to fortnightly is more effective. Traditional Thai massage combines assisted stretching, acupressure, and work along the body's sen energy lines. Flexibility and range of motion build up across sessions, so regular repetition makes a real difference.

Is it okay to get a massage every week?

Yes, for most people weekly massage is safe and helpful. This is especially true when managing chronic pain, high stress, or a busy training schedule. The exception is deep tissue massage. With this technique, it is best to allow at least one to two weeks between sessions so muscles can recover fully. If you are unsure, speak to your therapist about the right pace for you.

Can you get a massage too often?

Yes. Too many sessions — especially with high-pressure techniques — can tire muscles and slow recovery rather than help it. If soreness lasts more than two days after a session, you may need more time between appointments. Lighter techniques like relaxation or oil massage can be received more often without issue. Deep tissue work needs more space between sessions.

How often should athletes get a sports massage?

During a normal training period, one sports massage every two to three weeks is a practical routine. In the lead-up to a competition or during heavy training, weekly sessions are common. A post-event massage within 24 to 48 hours of intense activity can also help recovery by reducing inflammation and improving blood flow to worked muscles.

Does massage frequency change if you have back pain?

Yes. Research on lower back pain has consistently used weekly massage over eight to ten weeks as the treatment model. Results tend to last even after the course ends. Starting with more frequent sessions and reducing them as pain improves is the standard approach. Massage for lower back pain works best as part of a steady programme rather than a one-off treatment.

What is the minimum frequency needed to see lasting benefits from massage?

For general wellness, most practitioners consider monthly massage the minimum to maintain results. Below this, the body tends to return to its baseline tension between sessions and the benefits are harder to hold on to. Even once a month has value. But if you want real change in flexibility, pain levels, or stress, fortnightly is a more effective target to aim for.
