The Real Side Effects of Thai Massage and What to Do About Them

Thai massage is one of the most physically involved bodywork techniques you'll encounter. Unlike a gentle Swedish oil massage, traditional Thai massage uses acupressure, assisted stretching, and pressure along the body's sen energy lines to release tension and restore mobility. Most people leave a session feeling lighter, more flexible, and genuinely restored. But for some, particularly after a first session or a deeper treatment, there are a few side effects worth knowing about before you book. None of them are reasons to avoid Thai massage. They're simply things to expect, and to understand, so you can look after yourself well afterwards.

Muscle Soreness in the Days After

The most common side effect of Thai massage is mild muscle soreness. You'll usually feel it in the 24 to 48 hours after a session. It happens for the same reason you feel sore after a good workout — the muscles have been worked, stretched, and stimulated in ways they're not used to.

This is especially common after your first Thai massage, or after a deep treatment on areas of long-held tension. The soreness is generally mild and fades on its own within a day or two.

Gentle movement, plenty of water, and a warm (not hot) shower can help it pass more quickly. At Glasgow Thai Massage, our practitioners always tailor pressure to your comfort level. If you're new to traditional Thai massage, letting your therapist know means they can take a gentler approach and reduce the chance of soreness afterwards.

Fatigue and Grogginess

Feeling tired after Thai massage is very common, and often catches first-timers off guard. The session asks a lot of your body — deep acupressure work engages the nervous system, and the assisted stretching is genuinely physical, much like a yoga session in terms of effort.

When the body finally lets go of built-up tension, it can respond with a wave of tiredness. It shifts into rest and repair mode. This is not a bad sign — it means the treatment has done its job.

The practical advice: don't book a Thai massage before anything that needs high energy or sharp focus. An evening slot, or one you follow with a quiet afternoon, tends to work best. You can book a session online and choose a time that fits your schedule and recovery plan.

Temporary Headaches

Some people get a mild headache in the hours after Thai massage. This is more likely if the treatment involved work around the neck, upper back, or shoulders. Tight muscles in these areas can release quickly and briefly affect the surrounding tissue.

Dehydration can also play a role. Massage boosts circulation and helps move waste products through the body, so if you haven't had enough water before or after your session, a headache is more likely.

Drinking water before and after your treatment is one of the simplest ways to prevent this. A Thai head massage, which focuses on the scalp, neck, and upper shoulders, is actually used by many clients to relieve tension headaches rather than cause them. Technique and touch matter a great deal here.

Bruising or Skin Sensitivity

In rarer cases, Thai massage can leave some light bruising or skin tenderness. This is more likely for people with sensitive skin or those who bruise easily, and tends to happen where firm pressure has been applied for a sustained period.

If you know you bruise easily, mention it to your therapist before the session. A well-trained practitioner will adjust their approach. At Glasgow Thai Massage, both Maliwan and Jariya have years of experience reading how the body responds to pressure — they'll adapt in real time.

Who Should Take Extra Care Before Booking

Thai massage is safe for most people. But in some cases, it's worth checking with your GP first. In others, it's simply a matter of telling your therapist before the session begins.

These cases include recent surgery or fractures, active inflammation or acute injuries, heart conditions, blood clotting disorders, and pregnancy. Women who are pregnant are generally advised to avoid traditional Thai massage in the first trimester, and specialist advice is recommended before any bodywork in later stages.

For sports injury recovery, a Thai sports massage — targeted and specific — may suit better than a full-body traditional session. The key point is communication. A good therapist won't just proceed regardless. They'll ask, listen, and adapt — and that's what separates a quality treatment from a generic one.

Knowing the side effects of Thai massage doesn't reduce its value. It makes you a more informed client. And a more informed client gets more from every session. If you've been curious about trying Thai massage but held back by doubt, here's the honest answer: the side effects are mild, manageable, and mostly avoidable with a little care. For most people, the question isn't whether to try it. It's when to book the next one.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it normal to feel sore after a Thai massage?

Yes, mild muscle soreness after Thai massage is common and completely normal. It's more likely after a first session or a deeper treatment. The acupressure and assisted stretching work the muscles in ways that can feel similar to a workout. The soreness usually fades within 24 to 48 hours. Drinking plenty of water and doing gentle movement the day after your session can help it pass more quickly.

Can Thai massage cause a headache?

A mild headache after Thai massage is possible, especially if the session focused on the neck, shoulders, or upper back. Releasing tension in these areas can briefly affect the surrounding muscles. Dehydration is also a contributing factor, so drinking water before and after your treatment helps. Headaches of this kind are short-lived and usually clear within a few hours.

How long do the side effects of Thai massage last?

Most side effects — including muscle soreness, fatigue, and mild skin sensitivity — pass within 24 to 48 hours. Tiredness often fades overnight. If any discomfort lasts beyond two days or feels severe, it's worth contacting your therapist or GP. For most clients, side effects are minor and brief. The benefits of better flexibility, reduced tension, and improved circulation far outweigh any short-term discomfort.

Who should avoid Thai massage?

Thai massage isn't suitable for everyone in all situations. People with recent fractures, active inflammation, blood clotting conditions, or certain heart conditions should check with their GP before booking. Pregnant women, especially in the first trimester, are generally advised to avoid traditional Thai massage. If you have a health condition or are recovering from an injury, let your therapist know before your session. They can adjust the treatment or suggest a better alternative.

What can I do to reduce side effects after a Thai massage?

The most useful steps are simple. Drink plenty of water before and after your session. Avoid caffeine and alcohol for a few hours after treatment. Rest rather than going straight back to intense activity. Skip the very hot shower immediately afterwards — heat can increase inflammation in muscles that have already been worked. If you feel sore, gentle movement and a lukewarm shower are better choices than sitting still or applying heat. Talking openly with your therapist about pressure during the session also helps reduce the risk of discomfort afterwards.
