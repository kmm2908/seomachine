How Glasgow's New Walking and Cycling Routes Are Changing the Way We Exercise

Most people in Glasgow weren't getting enough movement into their day, and for years the city's roads made active travel feel more like a gamble than a lifestyle choice. That's changing fast. A wave of new infrastructure is reshaping streets across the city, and the knock-on effect for everyday fitness is bigger than most people realise.

A City Network Taking Shape

Glasgow City Council is building a 270km City Network of safe, segregated active travel routes. This isn't a few painted lines on busy roads. We're talking about dedicated cycleways, upgraded footways, redesigned junctions, and better street lighting across the city.

Routes like the South West City Way, the East City Way, and the Connecting Greater Govan corridor are already open and in use. The George V Bridge now has new pedestrian crossings and segregated cycleways. Work is moving forward along St George's Road in Woodside. More phases are planned for Battlefield, Yorkhill, and Kelvingrove.

It is, by any measure, the most ambitious active travel investment Glasgow has seen. And people are actually using it. If you want to make a regular habit of it, you can book a session at Glasgow Thai Massage to support what your body is putting in on the routes.

The Numbers Tell the Story

Cycling on Glasgow's new routes has surged. Counters on the South West City Way recorded 13,487 cycle journeys in September 2025. That's up from 6,241 in September 2021 — a rise of 116%. Southside routes combined logged over 131,000 cycle journeys in a single month. That's nearly double the figure from four years earlier.

The Glasgow Walking and Cycling Index 2025 found growing confidence among residents using local streets for active travel. That confidence tracks directly with the new infrastructure. When routes feel safe, people use them.

At a national level, the same index found that walking, wheeling, and cycling prevented around 150,000 long-term health conditions across the UK. It also saved the NHS close to £2 billion in 2025 alone. Glasgow is playing a real part in that.

What This Means for Your Fitness Routine

The strongest sign that someone will exercise regularly is convenience. When the route is safe, direct, and easy to use, people build movement into their day without thinking of it as a workout.

That's the quiet value of what Glasgow is building. A regular 30-minute cycle commute on a segregated route does more for heart health than most gym memberships that go unused by February. For people who find it hard to set aside exercise time, infrastructure like this removes the biggest barrier: friction.

The flip side is that more activity places real demands on the body. Cycling several miles a day — especially if you're starting from scratch — will tighten hip flexors, load the lower back, and put steady pressure on the knees. That's not a reason to stop. It's a reason to look after yourself alongside it.

Making the Most of Glasgow's Changing Streets

Whether you're cycling to work along the South Side routes, walking through Kelvingrove, or exploring the new Avenues on foot, the habits you're building now matter. Start gradually, vary your routes, and notice how your body responds to new demands.

Glasgow is putting the infrastructure in place. The city is becoming more walkable and cycleable, and the health case for using it is clear. Pairing that activity with good recovery — sleep, nutrition, or hands-on bodywork — is simply what keeps it going long term.

They share techniques but differ in focus. Traditional Thai massage is a full-body session working through a set sequence of stretches, joint work, and acupressure. Thai sports massage is more targeted — it focuses on specific muscle groups under load from your activity, whether that's cycling, running, or gym work. For someone using Glasgow's active travel routes regularly, a sports massage works well for a specific area of tightness or soreness. Traditional Thai massage is better for a full reset and mobility session.
