## Content Perspective

This is the strategic "why" behind all content we create. Reference it to ensure every piece has a point of view, not just information.

**What we believe needs to change in the industry:**
Massage therapy is consistently undersold. Most businesses — and most content — frame it as relaxation or pampering. This misrepresents what skilled therapeutic massage actually does and creates a mental barrier: clients feel they need to justify the spend rather than recognising it as a routine investment in their physical and mental health.

**How this change benefits our audience:**
When people understand that massage addresses real physiological issues — muscle tension, postural strain, stress-driven inflammation, disrupted sleep — they make better decisions about their health. They book before they're in crisis, not after.

**How Serendipity contributes to that change:**
Serendipity is a professional team practice, not a sole trader or a chain. Every therapist is trained to a consistent standard using techniques developed by Jariya Malone. Our content should communicate that standard and position Serendipity as the go-to for people who want results, not just an hour off.

**In practice, this means:**
- Frame treatments around what they do, not just how they feel
- Address specific conditions and audiences — don't write for "anyone who needs a massage"
- Emphasise the team's training and consistency as a differentiator from both independent therapists and chain spa brands
- Make regularity the goal — content should build the case for ongoing sessions, not just the first visit

## Brand Voice Pillars

### 1. Warm and Welcoming
- **What it means**: Every client deserves unhurried, personal attention — not a conveyor belt.
- **How it sounds**: Inclusive, calm, and direct. We speak to the person, not at them.
- **Example**: "Whether it's your first time or you've been visiting us for years, every session begins with the same intention: to leave you feeling better than when you arrived."
- **Avoid**: Transactional or clinical language; corporate wellness jargon.

### 2. Professional and Credible
- **What it means**: Serendipity is committed to training exceptional therapists. This isn't a pop-up or a chain. Every therapist is professional, qualified, and trained to the same standard.
- **How it sounds**: Confident and specific — without being boastful. Reference technique and training where it adds genuine weight.
- **Example**: "Our signature techniques, developed by head therapist Jariya Malone, draw from within the traditional Thai massage tradition — adapted to deliver precise, consistent results."
- **Avoid**: Vague wellness platitudes; claims the business can't support.

### 3. Holistic, Not Clinical
- **What it means**: We treat the whole person — physical tension, mental stress, and the need for genuine rest.
- **How it sounds**: Thoughtful and considered. Acknowledges that clients carry more than a sore neck through the door.
- **Example**: "A session at Serendipity isn't just time away from your desk. It's time your body has been asking for."
- **Avoid**: Over-medicalising. Also avoid going too far into spiritual or mystical territory — keep it grounded and real.

### 4. Local and Specific
- **What it means**: We are a Glasgow business, on Hope Street, serving people who live and work in this city.
- **How it sounds**: References real places, realistic commutes, and the kinds of lives people in Glasgow actually live.
- **Example**: "For anyone working in the offices around Blythswood Square or St Vincent Street, we're a short walk from your working day."
- **Avoid**: Generic "city centre" language that could apply to any UK city.

## Tone Guidelines

### General Tone
Imagine a highly skilled therapist who loves their work and wants clients to genuinely benefit. Warm, direct, unhurried. They don't oversell. When they explain a treatment, it's in plain language — not jargon aimed at impressing other therapists. The tone of someone you'd trust immediately.

### Tone by Content Type

**Service pages**: Reassuring and informative. Reduce pre-booking anxiety. Answer questions before they're asked. Warm but professional.

**Location pages**: Conversational and locally specific. Speak to the area — who lives and works there, what their day looks like, why they'd benefit.

**Blog posts**: Friendly and knowledgeable. More personal than service pages. Can have a slight opinion or angle. A trusted expert writing for a general audience.

**FAQ answers**: Clear, direct, practical. Short sentences. No filler.

## Terminology

**Say This** → **Not That**

- Thai massage → oriental massage (outdated, inaccurate)
- Therapist / practitioner → masseuse (cheapens the profession)
- Session / treatment → service / procedure (too transactional)
- Assisted stretching → passive stretching (less descriptive)
- Booked in / appointment → slot (impersonal)

## Writing Rules

These rules apply to all content — blog articles, landing pages, geo pages, and everything else.

### Rule: No hyphens or dashes as sentence connectors

Do not use hyphens (`-`), en-dashes, or em-dashes to connect clauses, introduce asides, or stand in for proper punctuation. Use commas, colons, semicolons, or full stops instead.

**Bad:** `The area draws professionals — lawyers, architects, creatives — who work long hours.`
**Good:** `The area draws professionals: lawyers, architects, and creatives who work long hours.`

**Bad:** `It's a simple treatment - and one that works.`
**Good:** `It's a simple treatment, and one that works.`

Hyphens are only permitted for compound words (e.g. well-being, deep-tissue, city-centre).

### Rule: Business name near service keyword phrases

When a keyword phrase like "Thai massage in [area]" or "[service] in [area]" appears in body text, the actual business name must appear at least once nearby (within two sentences).

**Bad:** `Thai massage in the West End fits into this picture precisely because...`
**Good:** `Serendipity Massage Therapy brings Thai massage to the West End precisely because...`
