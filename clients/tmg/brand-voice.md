## Content Perspective

**What we believe needs to change in the industry:**
Massage therapy is consistently undersold. Most businesses frame it as relaxation or pampering. This misrepresents what skilled therapeutic massage actually does and creates a mental barrier: clients feel they need to justify the spend rather than recognising it as a routine investment in their physical and mental health.

**How this change benefits our audience:**
When people understand that massage addresses real physiological issues (muscle tension, postural strain, stress-driven inflammation, disrupted sleep) they make better decisions about their health. They book before they're in crisis, not after.

**How Thai Massage Greenock contributes to that change:**
Thai Massage Greenock is a therapist-led practice, not a chain or franchise. Jariya personally delivers every treatment using techniques learned at Wat Po and refined through years of practice. Content should communicate that authenticity and position Thai Massage Greenock as the go-to for people in Inverclyde who want results, not just an hour off.

**In practice, this means:**
- Frame treatments around what they do, not just how they feel
- Address specific conditions and audiences
- Emphasise Jariya's Wat Po training and personalised approach as differentiators
- Make regularity the goal: content should build the case for ongoing sessions, not just the first visit

## Brand Voice Pillars

### 1. Warm and Personal
- **What it means**: Every client is known and remembered. Jariya takes detailed notes and builds on previous sessions.
- **How it sounds**: Genuine, caring, and direct. Like talking to someone who genuinely wants to help.
- **Example**: "Whether it's your first visit or you've been coming for years, every session starts with a conversation about how you're feeling today."
- **Avoid**: Corporate language; impersonal tone; generic wellness speak.

### 2. Authentic and Credible
- **What it means**: Real training, real expertise. Not a franchise following a script.
- **How it sounds**: Confident and specific without being boastful. Reference Wat Po training and Thai tradition where it adds genuine weight.
- **Example**: "Trained at Bangkok's world-famous Wat Po temple, Jariya brings authentic Thai technique to every treatment."
- **Avoid**: Vague wellness platitudes; claims the business can't support.

### 3. Holistic, Not Clinical
- **What it means**: We treat the whole person: physical tension, mental stress, and the need for genuine rest.
- **How it sounds**: Thoughtful and considered. Acknowledges that clients carry more than a sore neck through the door.
- **Example**: "A session at Thai Massage Greenock isn't just time away from your routine. It's time your body has been asking for."
- **Avoid**: Over-medicalising. Also avoid going too far into spiritual or mystical territory. Keep it grounded and real.

### 4. Local and Specific
- **What it means**: We are a Greenock business on South Street, serving people who live and work in Inverclyde and the surrounding area.
- **How it sounds**: References real places, real commutes, and the kinds of lives people in Inverclyde actually live.
- **Example**: "Just off the main shopping area on South Street, we're easy to find whether you're coming from Gourock, Port Glasgow, or anywhere in Inverclyde."
- **Avoid**: Generic language that could apply to any Scottish town.

## Tone Guidelines

### General Tone
Imagine a highly skilled therapist who loves their work and wants clients to genuinely benefit. Warm, direct, unhurried. They don't oversell. When they explain a treatment, it's in plain language. The tone of someone you'd trust immediately.

### Tone by Content Type

**Service pages**: Reassuring and informative. Reduce pre-booking anxiety. Answer questions before they're asked.

**Location pages**: Conversational and locally specific. Speak to the area, who lives and works there, why they'd benefit.

**Blog posts**: Friendly and knowledgeable. More personal than service pages. Can have a slight opinion or angle.

**FAQ answers**: Clear, direct, practical. Short sentences. No filler.

## Terminology

**Say This** → **Not That**

- Thai massage → oriental massage (outdated, inaccurate)
- Therapist / practitioner → masseuse (cheapens the profession)
- Session / treatment → service / procedure (too transactional)
- Assisted stretching → passive stretching (less descriptive)
- Booked in / appointment → slot (impersonal)

## Writing Rules

These rules apply to all content.

### Rule: No hyphens or dashes as sentence connectors

Do not use hyphens (`-`), en-dashes, or em-dashes to connect clauses, introduce asides, or stand in for proper punctuation. Use commas, colons, semicolons, or full stops instead.

**Bad:** `The area draws professionals — lawyers, architects, creatives — who work long hours.`
**Good:** `The area draws professionals: lawyers, architects, and creatives who work long hours.`

Hyphens are only permitted for compound words (e.g. well-being, deep-tissue).

### Rule: Business name near service keyword phrases

When a keyword phrase like "Thai massage in [area]" or "[service] in [area]" appears in body text, the actual business name must appear at least once nearby (within two sentences).

**Bad:** `Thai massage in Greenock fits into this picture precisely because...`
**Good:** `Thai Massage Greenock brings authentic Thai massage to Inverclyde precisely because...`
