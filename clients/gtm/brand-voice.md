## Brand Voice Pillars

### 1. Warm and Welcoming
- **What it means**: Every visitor is greeted as someone deserving care and attention, not just a booking slot.
- **How it sounds**: Inclusive, unhurried, and personal. We speak directly to the person, not at them.
- **Example**: "Whether you're stepping in for the first time or you've been coming to us for years, every session starts with the same intention: to help you feel better than when you arrived."
- **Avoid**: Transactional language, clinical detachment, long-winded explanations with fancy words and corporate formality.

### 2. Grounded in Expertise
- **What it means**: This is not a chain or a pop-up. Maliwan trained at Wat Pho in Bangkok and brings over 20 years of practice. That background shapes everything.
- **How it sounds**: Confident, specific, and credible — without being boastful. Reference technique and training when it adds weight.
- **Example**: "Traditional Thai massage works along the body's sen energy lines — a system of pathways that, when clear, support circulation, flexibility, and ease of movement."
- **Avoid**: Vague wellness platitudes. Claims that can't be backed by the practice's actual methods.

### 3. Holistic, Not Clinical
- **What it means**: We treat the whole person — physical tension, mental stress, and the need for genuine rest — not just an aching shoulder.
- **How it sounds**: Thoughtful and considered. Recognises that most clients carry more than a sore back through the door.
- **Example**: "A session at Glasgow Thai Massage isn't just an hour away from your inbox. It's an hour where your body is given the full attention it's been asking for."
- **Avoid**: Over-medicalising. We are not a physio clinic. Also avoid going too far into spiritual/mystical territory — keep it grounded.

### 4. Local and Specific
- **What it means**: We are a Glasgow business, in Glasgow City Centre, serving Glasgow people. Content should feel local, not generic.
- **How it sounds**: References real places, realistic commutes, and the kinds of lives people in this city actually live.
- **Example**: "For anyone working in the offices around Blythswood Square or the financial district on St Vincent Street, we're a short walk from the end of your working day."
- **Avoid**: Generic "city centre" language that could apply to any UK city.

## Tone Guidelines

### General Tone
Imagine a highly skilled practitioner who genuinely loves their work and wants clients to get real benefit from every visit. They're warm, direct, and unhurried. They don't oversell — their work speaks for itself. When they explain what a treatment does, it's in plain language that makes sense to someone who's never had a Thai massage, not jargon aimed at impressing other therapists.

### Tone by Content Type

**Service pages**: Reassuring and informative. The goal is to reduce pre-booking anxiety. Answer questions before they're asked. Warm but professional.

**Geo/location pages**: Conversational and locally specific. Speak to the area — who lives and works there, what their day looks like, why they'd benefit from an hour away.

**Blog category priority** (most to least frequent publishing):
1. **Thai Massage** — benefits, techniques, conditions, mental wellness. Primary focus.
2. **Stay Healthy** — nutrition, wellbeing habits, general health tips. Complements the massage angle.
3. **Yoga** — poses, pregnancy yoga, flexibility, mindfulness. Related wellness content.
4. **Glasgow News** — local events, city highlights, neighbourhood stories. Builds local relevance.

**Blog posts**: Friendly and knowledgeable. More personal than service pages. Can have a slight opinion or angle. The tone of a trusted expert writing for a general audience.

**FAQ answers**: Clear, direct, practical. Short sentences. No filler.

## Terminology

**Say This** → **Not That**

- Thai massage → oriental massage (outdated, inaccurate)
- Practitioner / therapist → masseuse (cheapens the profession)
- Sen energy lines → meridians (incorrect tradition)
- Session / treatment → service / procedure (too transactional)
- Assisted stretching → passive stretching (less descriptive)
- Booked in / appointment → slot / slot available (impersonal)

## Writing Rules

These rules apply to all content — blog articles, landing pages, geo pages, and everything else.

### Rule: No hyphens or dashes as sentence connectors

Do not use hyphens (`-`), en-dashes, or em-dashes to connect clauses, introduce asides, or stand in for proper punctuation. Use commas, colons, semicolons, or full stops instead.

**Bad:** `The area draws professionals — lawyers, architects, creatives — who work long hours.`
**Good:** `The area draws professionals: lawyers, architects, and creatives who work long hours.`

**Bad:** `It's a simple treatment - and one that works.`
**Good:** `It's a simple treatment, and one that works.`

Hyphens are only permitted for compound words (e.g. well-being, deep-tissue, city-centre).

### Rule: Business name near service keyword phrases

When a keyword phrase like "Thai massage in [area]" or "[service] in [area]" appears in body text, the actual business name must appear at least once nearby (within two sentences) so the reader understands the business is the provider, not just the category.

**Bad:** `Thai massage in Finnieston fits into this picture precisely because...`
**Good:** `Glasgow Thai Massage brings Thai massage to Finnieston precisely because...`
**Also good:** `Thai massage in Finnieston, delivered by Glasgow Thai Massage, fits into this picture...`
